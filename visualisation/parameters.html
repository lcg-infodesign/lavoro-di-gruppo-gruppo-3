Visualisation About Parameters

These parameters reflect various aspects of gender equality and the status of women in society, providing a multi-dimensional view of women’s socioeconomic, cultural, and political status globally, helping identify areas where gender disparities persist and where progress has been made.

Economic Rights
House ownership: measures the percentage of women who own a house, indicating women's access to assets and financial independence.
Economic Rights
Land ownership: Indicates the proportion of women owning land, reflecting economic empowerment and inheritance rights.
Economic Rights
Bank account ownership: tracks women's access to formal financial systems, essential for autonomy and participation in the economy.
Freedom & Justice
Feeling of safety at night: indicates women’s perception of public safety, a key factor for mobility and freedom.
Freedom & Justice
Confidence in the Court and Judicial System: indicates trust in legal institutions to protect women's rights and ensure justice.
Freedom & Justice
Perception that men aren’t necessarily better political leaders than women: measures beliefs about women’s leadership capabilities, crucial for political representation.
General Violence
Women who didn’t undergo child marriage: Shows the percentage of women married after the legal or socially accepted age, highlighting progress in protecting girls’ rights.
General Violence
Women who didn’t undergo female genital mutilation (FGM): measures the prevalence of FGM avoidance, an important marker of bodily autonomy and health rights.
General Violence
Perception that FGM should stop: reflects societal awareness and rejection of harmful traditional practices.
General Violence
Perception that a husband not justified in hitting or beating his wife under any circumstances: highlights attitudes toward domestic violence and gender-based abuse.
Social Rights
Perception that children don’t necessarily suffer more if a mother works for pay: reflects attitudes toward working mothers, challenging stereotypes about traditional gender roles.
Social Rights
Perception that a woman earning more/the same as her husband doesn’t cause problems: gauges societal acceptance of women's economic empowerment within households.
Social Rights
Perception that men don't necessarily make better business executives than women: captures gender biases in professional leadership and workplace equality.
Social Rights
Perception that men shouldn't have more right to a job than women: highlights societal attitudes toward gender equality in employment.
